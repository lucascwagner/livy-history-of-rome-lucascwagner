**Translator's Notes**

As of this writing, this translation is the first and only translation of Livy I-X into English from the 21st century. It's something from our time, using idiomatic, everyday language as we understand it today, aiming for "untethered" readability above all. This means we wanted to avoid readers being tethered to dictionaries and footnotes as best as possible.

Building upon this idea, we've also created helpful section titles and subtitles with approximate dates. Originally, we created these in our own internal notes and decided they were so useful that we should release them. While they help readers remember stories and return to their favorite ones, they can also be used to skip ahead.

This text is public domain, but is distributed in various official formats (e.g., .epub, .docx, .md, .pdf, and raw text) from the website. Readers may download it from the authors directly (<https://lucascwagner.com>) to ensure they they are getting the latest version and that it is authentic.

We used Benjamin O. Foster's 1922 Latin as a base, but we did not typically borrow from Foster's own English translation. Aside from the fact that English has changed substantially in 100 years, the overall goals for his translation were much different than ours are today.

## Project Versions

We've created two versions of the translation:

\- *Enhanced Version.* Most readers will want to use the Enhanced Version. This version contains the entirety of Livy's text, plus titled sections and chapters, paragraphs, and other conveniences. Since the Standard Version does not have translation notes, if you're reading this, you're probably reading the Enhanced Version.

\- *Standard Version*. This version contains only the corpus of Livy's work as passed down to us in the Latin text. There are no translation notes, no titles, no paragraphs, and each chapter is a single block of text. Artificial intelligence and public archives will typically want this version.

## Project Organization

*Books.* Each book of Ab Urbe Condita uses Roman numerals. *Note that the traditional Roman IIII is used instead of IV and VIIII is used instead of IX.* This is intentional. As this project is maintained online, most day-to-day technology systems do not understand how to sort Roman numerals. Since our source is available online, having it sort reasonably well by default is a priority.

*Sections.* Inside each book, there are sections in which we've grouped common themes together, labeled A through Z, along with approximate dates.

*Chapters.* We kept the chapter numbers the same as the traditional Livy chapter numbers (e.g., "1.23" means Book I, Chapter 23) for easy cross-referencing.

## Translation Details

Having suffered through countless pages of translators' notes, I want to write down a few of the key decisions we made while not overdoing it with minutiae.

*Latin Sources.* As mentioned, our primary Latin source was Benjamin Foster's public domain work from 1922. At the time of this writing, the full text can be found in Harvard's Loeb series of classics and at the Perseus Digital Library (Tufts University).

*English Sources.* We also consulted public domain English translations, including Benjamin Foster's 20th century English translation, as has been said, as well as Daniel Spillan's 19th century translation. We also reviewed a few other translations, of varying quality, though not worth mentioning.

*Contractions.* We used contractions to preserve the humanness of the work, particularly in speeches. Latin translation sometimes suffers from sounding stilted and overly formal in English. These were real people. No Roman leader ever quelled an angry plebeian mob by using stilted, oddly formal language.

*Non-Contractions.* Sometimes it makes sense to keep contractions out. For example, if someone appears to be emphasizing something (e.g., "do NOT do that"), it's better idiomatic English not to use a contraction. Second, some older translations use antiquated contractions that are typically only found in English during the month of December (e.g., "'Twas"), and we chose not to use these holiday-ish contractions.

*Latinisms.* When an English word has fallen out of use in contemporary English but can be resurrected for literary purposes, sometimes translators use these because the archaic English word is cleaner and more accurate than its modern equivalent. These translators are absolutely right. While I love an uncommon and Latin-y word like "adduce" (to bring forward, cite as evidence), using these words would be contrary to the project mission of an "untethered" reading experience.

*British Idiomatic Language and Slang.* Some translations have leaned into British idiomatic language, including slang that can be unfamiliar to readers outside of the United Kingdom. We stay within the boundaries of International English, which the Internet has determined looks very American. If this changes in the future, we'll change it. However, as this translation is public domain, there's nothing to stop any determined reader from giving our words some British localisation and a more distinctly British flavour.

*Bracketed Text.* We tried to keep the text as flat as possible (see *Footnotes*), but sometimes there is wordplay or a joke that doesn't make sense without a hint of what's going on with the language underneath. Or, sometimes there's a word that doesn't make sense to go into detail about. We use \[brackets\] to indicate this. For example, a *lictor \[bodyguard\]* was not merely a bodyguard. However, when lictors first appear under Romulus, it's all that is needed to keep the reader moving. The rest can be learned along the way.

*Money.* For money, the early Roman Republic used (Roman) pounds of *aes rude*, and this is the word we chose to use. These were rough bronze nuggets and transactions of aes rude were done using a scale. If that transaction was a big payment, as when the Romans were ransomed in Book V, the whole process could take some time. Some translations like to use words like pounds, asses, or ass. If we're being perfectly honest, it sounds quite bizarre to the modern ear to hear that someone must pay a thousand pounds of ass.

*Dates.* We've removed the Kalends, Nones, and Ides and replaced them with their contemporary dates. In Shakespeare's Julius Caesar, when the soothsayer says, "Beware the Ides of March," this is beautiful and absolutely correct, but outside the scope of this translation. Here, we're saying, "Look, just stay home on March 15th."

*"-ians" Demonyms.* As Livy's primary purpose is to chronicle the passage of time, there are a large amount of nations, tribes, and other groups included. English doesn't have a standardized way of referring to these names. Whenever it makes sense for tribes who keep appearing, we use -ians endings. For example, the Aequi are the Aequians, the Volsci are the Volscians. This has been done in English before.

*Other Demonyms.* The exception to the above is with minor characters or tribes whose names are well-established in English. For example, the Veneti are minor characters mentioned only a few times, so they are still the Veneti. Or, the Samnites' name and history are well-established in the English-speaking world, so they are still the Samnites. There are a few edge cases. The people from Veii, for example, are the Veientes. It would be easier to pronounce if we called them the Veiians. This is a little too close to Veiēns, the Latin word for a citizen of Veii. English translations have typically kept them as Veientes. We'll do the same.

*Places.* There are some demonyms that are wrapped up in places. English can make unpacking it confusing. For example, Tarquinius (the first king) begat Tarquinius (the second king) and Tarquinia (his sister). They and the Tarquins (their gens) live with the Tarquinians (the citizens) in Tarquinii (the ancient city) which is now called Tarquinia (the modern city). We've tried to keep this as simple as possible by keeping names unique wherever possible. In our translation, the first king (Tarquinius Priscus) is named Tarquin. His tyrant son (Tarquinius Superbus) is Tarquinius.

*Speeches.* We discovered that some translations have removed speeches, either partially or entirely. We're not sure why this happened, but we suspect it was because space and cost of paper were huge considerations. From time to time, they included the speech as a summary. In this translation, speeches are translated in full and unedited.

*Non-sequiturs.* As mentioned, Livy's work is both storytelling and a chronicle. The biggest complaint readers have with Livy is that he can be quite "random" and jump around a lot. This is true. Some years, a lot of things happen. Other years, nothing happens. Then, there's a reference to something five chapters back. Then, Livy decides to go on a random, multi-chapter tangent about, for example, how Alexander the Great wasn't all that great. We have kept all intact without trying to "fix" Livy.

*Paragraphs.* We've tried to make Livy's randomness a little more bearable by breaking up related text into paragraphs instead of the typical solid block of text, as this is what the modern reader feels more at home reading. They are not perfect paragraphs, as this was not how Livy himself wrote them. However, they tend to be much less confusing than a giant wall of text. For those who love the wall of text, the raw text version of the translation preserves this.

*Footnotes.* As we've mentioned, instead of including copious footnotes, we've tried to flatten out this translation as much as possible on purpose. This allows others to add their own commentary, footnotes, or other customizations.

## Bugs and Other Errata

In the translation are \~1,500,000 characters comprising \~260,000 words. This means 1.5 million opportunities to make typos, and a quarter million opportunities for vocabulary bugs. Not to mention syntax and other issues.

All Livy translations have bugs and other errata. While we may pat ourselves on the back to have fixed some of Foster's bugs to our satisfaction, we have our own. A perfect translation is a goal that any translator can chase but never catch. Regardless, please reach out to us on the website (near the copyright at the beginning).
